Five Page Book

Ken Sparling

I tell Kitty I think I will be using the white bath towel this week instead of the blue one. She says she thinks this is a good idea. This worries me.

I feel like this must mean that Kitty thinks me having a good idea is a rare thing.

Or maybe she thinks I use the blue bath towel too much. Maybe she thinks the blue bath towel smells.

Or maybe she thinks the white bath towel is better than the blue bath towel. Maybe she thinks I should use the white bath towel all the time and never use the blue bath towel. But wouldn’t it come down to the same thing if I used the white bath towel all the time? Wouldn’t the white bath towel then begin to smell, same as the blue towel?

Except that I’m not even sure that the way the towel I use smells is even the issue. I mean, what is the issue here? Is it simply that I want to make Kitty happy? Is it that the harder I try to make Kitty happy, the more unhappy she seems to get? Is my trying to make Kitty happy the leading contributor to her unhappiness? Because that’s what it feels like sometimes.

I’ve been reading a lot of books lately.

For a while, I was reading the red one.

The red one had been sitting on my bookshelf in the basement for a long time.

Every time I glanced over at the bookshelf while I was down there in the basement, I saw the red book sitting there.

So finally one day I pulled it off the shelf and began to read it.

Why do things come out of my mouth when I don’t want them to?

How do these things get into my mouth in the first place, and why do I always let them out? I mean, I could always keep them in.

Technically, I guess they aren’t actually in my mouth until I say them. Technically, they are in my head, I suppose.

But the point is, whenever I let them come out of my head and into my mouth in the form of words, I come to regret it.

It isn’t that I don’t stand behind what I say. It’s just that certain words, or combinations of words, make me nervous. In fact, to be truthful, I think that all the words that come out of my mouth make me nervous.

I yawned.

I was putting off getting out of bed.

I needed to pee.

Out of the corner of my eye I could see the book I’d been reading last night before I went to sleep.

It was lying face down on my bedside table.

The wind was blowing.

I could hear it.

I had discovered recently that I could pull the curtains back a little with my foot without getting out of the chair I was sitting in, so I did that now.

My foot was up high, above the level of my head, due to the angle at which I had reclined the Tony Little reclining chair I was reclining in.

Outside, the sun was peeking out intermittently from behind the clouds, and the shadows from the tree branches blowing in the wind created a kind of light show that danced on the periphery of my vision.
